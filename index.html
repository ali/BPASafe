BPA Safe

Learn more about staying healthy by avoiding products with BPA.

What is BPA?

Bisphenol A (BPA) is an organic compound used to make plastics and aluminum cans. It is very dangerous to our health, but still used in the making of ordinary items today. It is an endocrine disruptor, meaning it disturbs the normal activities of natural hormones in the body. This can result in mammary or prostrate cancers, early puberty, reproductive problems, Down Syndrome, ADHD, and/or a decreased sperm count. It is cheap and easy to use in the making of plastics and aluminum cans, which is why it hasn't been banned yet.

Where is BPA?

Type 3 plastics

  • Food wrap
  • Vegetable oil packaging
  • Juice bottles
  • PVC
  • Medical tubing

Type 7 plastics

  • Layered/mixed plastics
  • Beverage (and baby milk) bottles
  • Electronic casing

Other

  • The inside linings of aluminum cans
  • Thermal paper
    • Faxes
    • Receipts
    • Movie tickets
  • Water bottles
  • Cigarette filters

In the United States

  • California has banned BPA from baby bottles and sippy cups (going into effect July 1, 2013)
  • Conneticuit has banned BPA from all reusable food and beverage containers (in effect from October 1, 2011) and receipt paper (going into effect October 1, 2013)
  • Delaware has banned BPA from baby bottles and sippy cups (going into effect January 1, 2012)
  • Maine has banned BPA from all reusable food and beverage containers (going into effect January 1, 2012)
  • Maryland has banned BPA from baby bottles and sippy cups (going into effect January 1, 2012) and infant formula containers (going into effect July 1, 2014)
  • Massachusetts has banned BPA from baby bottles and sippy cups (in effect from July 1, 2011)
  • Minnesota has banned BPA from baby bottles and sippy cups (in effect from January 1, 2011
  • New York has banned BPA from baby bottles, sippy cups, and pacifiers (in effect from December 1, 2010)
  • Vermont has banned BPA from all reusable food and beverage containers (going into effect July 1, 2012) and from infant formula containers and baby food jars (going into effect July 1, 2014)
  • Washington has banned BPA from baby bottles, sippy cups, and other reusable food containers meant for children under the age of three (in effect from July 1, 2011) and reusable sports bottles (going into effect July 1, 2012)
  • Wisconsin has banned BPA from baby bottles and sippy cups (in effect from June 2010)

Countries

  • The European Union has banned the manufacturing of BPA (in effect from March 1, 2011) and the marketing and market place of polycarbonate baby bottles containing BPA (in effect from June 1, 2011)
  • Canada has banned BPA from baby bottles (in effect from April 18, 2008)
  • France has banned BPA from all food containers meant for children under the age of three (going into effect 2013) and for everyone (going into effect 2014)
  • Sweden has banned BPA from the manufacturing of baby bottles (in effect from March 2011)
  • Turkey has banned BPA from all items directed to babies, including baby bottles (in effect from June 10, 2011)

How to avoid BPA

Here are some ways you can avoid BPA, and stop its production.

  • Buy BPA-Free water bottles, or better yet, buy aluminum bottles that you can always refill.
  • Buy BPA-Free baby bottles
  • Write a letter to your congressperson or leader asking them to vote for BPA to be banned from your state, if it hasnâ€™t been already.
    To find out if your state our country has banned BPA already, see "Has anyone banned BPA?".
  • Tell others about this site, so they can also make the right choices.
  • Donate to organizations that are also working to stop BPA. For a list of organizations, see the Organizations page.
